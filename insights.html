Studying medicine in Belarus: what CBSE and IGCSE students should know

For many CBSE and IGCSE students in Qatar with their sights set on a medical degree, the path forward usually splits into two hard options: extremely competitive local seats, or private medical colleges at a cost few families can absorb. Government medical universities in Belarus have become a practical third option, and Gomel State Medical University is one of the more established names in that route.

Why this option keeps coming up

The appeal isn't complicated. Belarusian government medical universities run MBBS-equivalent programs taught in English, with admission processes built around academic transcripts rather than a separate entrance exam. For a student who has already done the work through Grade 12 science, that removes one of the biggest bottlenecks in the usual medical-admission process.

  • No IELTS or TOEFL requirement for admission
  • No separate medical entrance exam — admission is transcript-based
  • Direct admission process, with visa issued on arrival
  • Tuition significantly lower than private medical colleges in most home countries
  • On-campus accommodation with halal food options available
  • Degrees recognised by MCI, PMDC and SLMC frameworks
  • Work and PR pathways available after graduation

What a family should actually verify

None of the above is worth acting on until three things are confirmed directly with the university: that the specific degree is currently listed with the relevant medical council in the student's home country, that the university's accreditation is active for the intake year, and what the total cost looks like once accommodation, food, and visa processing are added to tuition. A quotation that only shows tuition is an incomplete picture.

It's also worth asking early about the licensing exam the student will eventually need to sit at home after graduating abroad — this is a separate process from university admission, and planning for it starts years before the actual exam date.

Exam prep — 4 min read

CBSE and IGCSE exam changes: how to stay ready without the panic

Every time an exam board moves a date, cancels a session, or announces a revised syllabus, the same pattern plays out in group chats and family conversations: a wave of relief, followed a few weeks later by a scramble once the next set of dates is confirmed. That gap between "we have time now" and "the new date just dropped" is where most preparation momentum gets lost.

Build the routine around the syllabus, not the calendar

A study plan tied to a fixed exam date breaks the moment that date changes. A study plan tied to syllabus completion doesn't. Breaking each subject into topic blocks with their own internal checkpoints means a schedule shift changes the pace, not the plan itself.

Treat "extra time" as consolidation, not a pause

When exam sessions get pushed back, the instinct is to relax the pace. The stronger move is to use the additional weeks for past-paper practice and closing specific weak topics — the things that are hard to fit in during a normal, tighter timeline.

  • Keep a running list of topics marked "not yet confident," updated weekly
  • Treat every schedule announcement as a trigger to re-check the list, not restart it
  • Prioritise past papers over new content once core topics are covered
  • Keep parents looped into progress, not just dates — it reduces last-minute pressure at home

Choosing a small-class school: what "2–8 students per class" really changes

Small class sizes are one of the most common lines in a school brochure, and one of the least explained. Parents comparing options for grades 1 through 8 deserve a clearer picture of what actually shifts when a classroom goes from thirty students to under ten.

What genuinely improves

With fewer students per teacher, individual progress is easier to track in real time — not just at report-card time. Teachers can adjust pace per student rather than to the middle of the class, and quieter students get more actual speaking and participation time in a session.

What to ask before enrolling

Small class size is a structural feature, not a guarantee of quality on its own. It's worth asking specifically about teacher-to-student ratios across all subjects (not just the headline number), how progress is reported to parents — monthly reports are common, but the actual content of those reports varies a lot — and whether the curriculum is genuinely internationally benchmarked or just labelled as such.

  • Confirm the class size figure applies to core subjects, not just electives
  • Ask for a sample monthly progress report before enrolling
  • Check how the curriculum maps to Cambridge or an equivalent recognised framework
  • Ask how the school handles a student who's ahead or behind the class average

School ERP — digital transformation

A practical School ERP built around how your institution actually works

A School ERP should do more than replace spreadsheets. It should connect academic, administrative and financial workflows in one reliable system, while giving each user access only to the information and actions relevant to their role.

Daaris Global begins with a structured needs assessment. We study the institution's current processes, pain points, approval flows, reporting needs and future plans before confirming the solution scope. This helps ensure the platform fits the school instead of forcing the school to adapt to a generic product.

Major ERP capabilities

  • Admissions enquiries, applications, document checks, approvals and student registration
  • Central student and guardian profiles with academic, attendance and document history
  • Academic-year setup, classes, subjects, teacher allocation, timetables and lesson planning
  • Daily or period attendance, late arrivals, absence alerts and behaviour follow-up
  • Examinations, marks validation, grading rules, report cards, certificates and transcripts
  • Fee structures, invoices, instalments, receipts, discounts and outstanding-balance reporting

  • Employee records, leave approvals, attendance, training and document-expiry tracking
  • Library catalogue, issue and return workflows, reservations, fines and circulation reports
  • Management dashboards, operational reports and export to common document formats
